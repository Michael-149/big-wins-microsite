Economic research transformed this system into sophisticated market-based auctions and unlicensed spectrum sharing arrangements, generating over $233 billion in government revenues while enabling the mobile and Wi-Fi revolutions that touch hundreds of millions of Americans daily.

The transformation began conceptually with Ronald Coase's controversial 1959 proposal to treat spectrum as property that could be bought and sold. It took 35 years for this idea to reach implementation, requiring new auction designs by economists Paul Milgrom, Robert Wilson, and others that operationalized game theory into implementable policy. The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) held its first spectrum auctions in 1994, proving the concept viable. Since then, over 100 auctions have raised $233+ billion while allocating spectrum more efficiently to highest-value uses. Complementary reforms opened vast swaths of spectrum for unlicensed use (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth), generating enormous economic valueâ€”the 6 GHz band opening alone created an estimated $870 billion in economic value in 2023-2024.

Path to Impact

1959

Coase's Radical Proposal

Ronald Coase proposes treating spectrum as property that can be bought and soldâ€”initially dismissed as impractical

1984-1993

Internal Advocacy

FCC economists Kwerel & Williams build case for auctions; Congress authorizes experimental approach

1994

First Spectrum Auctions

FCC conducts inaugural auctions using Milgrom-Wilson simultaneous ascending design, proving concept viable

2008

700 MHz Auction Success

$19.6B raised in auction enabling 4G LTE deployment nationwide

2020

6 GHz Band Opening

FCC opens 1200 MHz for unlicensed Wi-Fi use, expanding capacity 5x and enabling Wi-Fi 6E/7

2023

$233B+ Total Impact

Over 100 auctions conducted, with 6 GHz unlicensed opening generating $870B economic value
