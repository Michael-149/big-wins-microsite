12:Tc26,<p>Knowledge brokering takes many institutional forms, each playing distinct roles in the translation process:</p>
<h3>Convening Organizations</h3>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> Center for Global Development (AMC), Project 88 (emissions trading), Yale symposium (Stern Review debate)</p>
<p>These organizations create sustained dialogue spaces where researchers, policymakers, and practitioners build shared understanding and trust. They don't just host one-off conferencesâ€”they facilitate ongoing engagement that shapes how evidence gets interpreted and applied. The Center for Global Development spent years convening working groups with vaccine manufacturers, donors, and health ministries before the AMC concept became reality.</p>
<h3>Scaling Entities</h3>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> PERTS (growth mindset), Behavioral Insights Team/SBST/ideas42 (nudges), Auctionomics (FCC spectrum)</p>
<p>Organizations specifically designed to operationalize research insights at scale. They handle the messy work of implementation: adapting interventions to different contexts, training practitioners, troubleshooting failures, and iterating on designs. PERTS didn't just publish Dweck's growth mindset researchâ€”they built teacher training programs, created classroom materials, and provided ongoing support for thousands of schools.</p>
<h3>Government Champions</h3>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> FCC economists (spectrum auctions), HUD partnerships (Moving to Opportunity), CDC/NIDA (needle exchanges)</p>
<p>Insiders who understand both the research and the policy process. They identify opportunities for evidence-based approaches, navigate institutional constraints, and advocate for change within bureaucracies. FCC economists didn't just read Milgrom and Wilson's papersâ€”they hired them as consultants, adapted auction designs to regulatory requirements, and championed the approach through internal decision-making processes.</p>
<h3>Think Tanks &#x26; Advocacy Groups</h3>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> Environmental Defense Fund (emissions trading), Wi-Fi Alliance (unlicensed spectrum), harm reduction advocates (needle exchanges)</p>
<p>Organizations that combine research translation with strategic advocacy, building political coalitions and public support. They frame evidence in ways that resonate with different stakeholders, identify policy windows, and sustain pressure for change. The Environmental Defense Fund didn't just cite academic papers on cap-and-tradeâ€”they drafted model legislation, testified before Congress, and built bipartisan support.</p>
<h3>Credible Messengers</h3>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> National Academies, Government Accountability Office, bipartisan coalitions, respected former officials</p>
<p>Trusted institutions that validate research findings and lower the perceived risk of adoption for policymakers. A National Academies report carries weight that individual studies don't. Recommendations from bipartisan commissions provide political cover. These messengers don't create new evidenceâ€”they certify existing evidence as reliable and actionable.</p>13:T496,<p>Knowledge brokers don't just write literature reviews. They transform research into artifacts that decision-makers can immediately use:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Policy-ready specifications:</strong> Model legislation, regulatory language, implementation guidelines</li>
<li><strong>Technical tools:</strong> Software code, auction algorithms, data platforms (like Opportunity Insights' Opportunity Atlas)</li>
<li><strong>Practitioner resources:</strong> Training materials, troubleshooting guides, implementation playbooks</li>
<li><strong>Decision metrics:</strong> Social Cost of Carbon estimates, cost-benefit analyses with standardized assumptions</li>
<li><strong>Risk assessments:</strong> What could go wrong, how to mitigate, what backup options exist</li>
</ul>
<p>The Center for Global Development didn't just endorse Advance Market Commitmentsâ€”they produced legal contract templates that vaccine manufacturers could review with their lawyers. Opportunity Insights didn't just show that neighborhoods matterâ€”they built interactive maps showing which neighborhoods provide upward mobility, making the research immediately actionable for housing authorities.</p>14:T544,<p>If the goal is real-world influence, identifying or creating knowledge brokering capacity should be part of the research strategyâ€”not an afterthought. This means:</p>
<p><strong>For researchers:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Identify existing brokers in your domain and build relationships early</li>
<li>Participate in policy dialogues, not just academic conferences</li>
<li>Create decision-ready artifacts, not just publications</li>
<li>Invest time in translation, even though it's unrewarded academically</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>For funders:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Support brokering organizations, not just researchers</li>
<li>Fund translation work: practitioner guides, policy briefs, toolkits</li>
<li>Build convenings that sustain dialogue between researchers and practitioners</li>
<li>Recognize that brokering capacity takes years to develop</li>
<li>Factor the absence of brokers into risk assessment when evaluating proposals</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Critical questions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Does effective brokering infrastructure exist for this research area?</li>
<li>If not, who could develop it, and what resources would that require?</li>
<li>Are there government champions, scaling organizations, or advocacy groups positioned to translate findings?</li>
<li>What artifacts would decision-makers need to act on this research?</li>
