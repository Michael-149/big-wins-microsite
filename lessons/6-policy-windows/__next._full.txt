<h3>Budget Pressures Change Political Calculus</h3>
<p>When the federal government faced budget shortfalls in the 1990s, spectrum allocation suddenly became a revenue question, not just a technical telecom regulation. <p>The research existed before the budget crisis. The crisis made it politically viable.</p>
<h3>Regulatory Requirements Create Sudden Demand</h3>
<p>Federal courts didn't just encourage climate cost analysisâ€”they <em>required</em> it. <h3>Political Transitions Reset Priorities</h3>
<p>New administrations, leadership changes, and electoral shifts create openness to approaches that previous leaders rejected. <h3>Focusing Events Concentrate Attention</h3>
<p>High-profile successes or failures focus political attention. The success of early emissions trading programs created appetite for cap-and-trade expansion. <h3>1. Political Will</h3>
<p>Decision-makers must be motivated to actâ€”not just interested in the research, but facing pressure to make decisions. <p>Broader constituencies must be ready to support change. Needle exchanges succeeded when communities faced HIV crises.
