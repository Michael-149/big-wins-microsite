16:T1ff2,<p>Each stage serves distinct functions, requires different capabilities, and faces different barriers:</p>
<h3>Stage 1: Genesis â€” Generate Ideas, Challenge Assumptions</h3>
<p><strong>Core Function:</strong> Produce conceptual breakthroughs that challenge existing paradigms and open new possibilities.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ronald Coase (1959):</strong> Challenged FCC spectrum allocation assumptions, proposing property rights and market mechanisms where command-and-control regulation had been unquestioned</li>
<li><strong>Kahneman &#x26; Tversky (1970s):</strong> Questioned rational choice models, documenting systematic deviations from economic rationality and establishing behavioral economics</li>
<li><strong>Early emissions trading theory (1970s):</strong> Proposed market-based environmental protection when regulation meant mandates, not markets</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Characteristics:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>High-risk work that might not have obvious applications for decades</li>
<li>Often challenges disciplinary orthodoxy or institutional practice</li>
<li>May be unfunded or supported only by basic research grants with no application requirements</li>
<li>Success measured by conceptual novelty and theoretical contribution</li>
<li>Individual or small-team efforts, limited stakeholder engagement</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key Challenge:</strong> Academic incentives reward genesis work, but most genesis never translates to impact. The question is which conceptual innovations merit continued investment.</p>
<h3>Stage 2: Proof of Concept â€” Test If It Actually Works</h3>
<p><strong>Core Function:</strong> Provide rigorous empirical evidence that an intervention or approach produces desired outcomes.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>New Haven needle exchange (1988):</strong> Documented HIV prevention without increasing drug use, countering moral objections with evidence</li>
<li><strong>Moving to Opportunity (1994-2010):</strong> Large-scale RCT testing whether neighborhood changes improve life outcomesâ€”initially showing mixed results, later revealing massive earnings impacts for young movers</li>
<li><strong>Early growth mindset experiments:</strong> Small-scale classroom studies demonstrating that mindset interventions improve academic persistence and achievement</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Characteristics:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Rigorous empirical work using RCTs, quasi-experiments, or robust observational designs</li>
<li>Tests core hypotheses but often in controlled or limited contexts</li>
<li>May not address implementation challenges or mechanisms</li>
<li>Success measured by statistical significance, effect sizes, and methodological rigor</li>
<li>Requires significant resources for data collection and analysis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key Challenge:</strong> Proof of concept establishes that something <em>can</em> work, not that it <em>will</em> work at scale under real-world constraints. Many validated interventions fail when implemented broadly.</p>
<h3>Stage 3: Mechanism â€” Explain Why It Works</h3>
<p><strong>Core Function:</strong> Uncover the processes and pathways through which interventions produce effects, identifying critical mechanisms and potential failure points.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Stefanie DeLuca's qualitative work:</strong> Revealed why Moving to Opportunity struggledâ€”families faced voucher acceptance deadlines, landlords resisted, families lacked search support. This mechanism research informed CMTO's design, transforming 15% success to 53%</li>
<li><strong>Growth mindset mechanism research:</strong> Identified how mindset shapes persistence, effort attribution, and response to failureâ€”understanding that enabled refinement of interventions</li>
<li><strong>Emissions trading studies (1980s):</strong> Explained why market approaches succeededâ€”flexibility enabled cost-effective abatement, trading created price discovery, monitoring ensured compliance</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Characteristics:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Combines qualitative and quantitative methods to understand processes</li>
<li>Fills critical gap between "it works" and "we understand how/why"</li>
<li>Often reveals barriers, enabling conditions, and context dependencies</li>
<li>Essential for troubleshooting failures and adapting to new contexts</li>
<li>May not generate high-prestige publications despite being critical for impact</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key Challenge:</strong> Academic incentives often undervalue mechanism researchâ€”journals prefer novel effects over understanding existing ones. Yet mechanism understanding determines whether interventions scale successfully.</p>
<h3>Stage 4: Design â€” Optimize for Real-World Implementation</h3>
<p><strong>Core Function:</strong> Transform validated concepts into implementable interventions that function in messy, resource-constrained, politically complex real-world environments.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Milgrom &#x26; Wilson FCC auction designs:</strong> Didn't just propose auctionsâ€”designed specific mechanisms addressing strategic delay, package bidding, collusion prevention, and default penalties. Each detail determined whether auctions generated billions while allocating spectrum efficiently</li>
<li><strong>Creating Moves to Opportunity (CMTO):</strong> Developed bundled support packagesâ€”customized search assistance, financial help for deposits, landlord engagement, barrier-specific problem-solving. Design work was the difference between 15% and 53% success</li>
<li><strong>Specific nudge interventions:</strong> Opt-out organ donation, default 401(k) enrollment, calorie disclosure formatsâ€”each required careful design of choice architecture, timing, framing, and feedback</li>
</ul>

<ul>
